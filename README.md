# CS 250 – Sprint Review and Retrospective

This repository contains my Sprint Review and Retrospective from **CS 250: Software Development**. In this project, I applied Scrum-Agile practices while rotating through the roles of Product Owner, Developer, Tester, and Scrum Master. This experience strengthened both my technical and collaborative skills and deepened my understanding of Agile development.

---

## Interpreting User Needs and Implementing Them into a Program

Interpreting user needs begins with understanding the *why* behind a request. Instead of focusing only on features, I focus on the problem the user is trying to solve and the value they expect to gain. During this project, I translated stakeholder requirements into structured user stories that followed the format:

> As a user, I want ___ so that ___.

Writing user stories helped me break large ideas into manageable, testable increments. Each story included acceptance criteria, which clarified what “done” meant and reduced ambiguity during development and testing. This approach ensured that implementation remained aligned with user value rather than simply completing technical tasks.

User stories also supported collaboration. Developers, testers, and the Product Owner all referenced the same criteria, which minimized miscommunication and improved efficiency across sprints.

---

## My Approach to Developing Programs

My development approach is iterative and incremental. Rather than attempting to design everything upfront, I focus on building small, functional increments that can be reviewed, tested, and improved. Throughout this course, I applied key Agile practices including:

- **Sprint Planning** to establish clear goals  
- **Daily Scrums** to maintain transparency and address blockers  
- **Backlog Refinement** to clarify and prioritize work  
- **Sprint Reviews** to gather feedback  
- **Sprint Retrospectives** to drive continuous improvement  

Moving forward, I plan to incorporate these Agile processes into my future development work. The structured rhythm of Scrum promotes accountability while remaining flexible enough to adapt to change. I especially value the emphasis on feedback and continuous improvement, as these practices reduce risk and increase product quality over time.

## What It Means to Be a Good Team Member in Software Development

Being a strong team member in software development means more than completing assigned tasks. It requires clear communication, reliability, adaptability, and respect for diverse perspectives. During this project, I learned that effective teamwork depends on shared ownership of outcomes rather than isolated contributions.

A good team member:

- Communicates progress and blockers transparently  
- Writes maintainable, well-documented code  
- Provides and receives constructive feedback  
- Supports teammates when challenges arise  
- Actively participates in Scrum events  

Rotating through multiple Scrum roles reinforced how interconnected each responsibility is. Developers rely on well-defined user stories, testers rely on clear acceptance criteria, and the Scrum Master relies on open communication. Success is a collective effort.

## Reflection on Agile and Professional Growth

This project strengthened my understanding of how Agile principles function in practice. I experienced firsthand how short iterations, structured communication, and adaptability support successful software delivery. I also developed a deeper appreciation for the importance of leadership, facilitation, and continuous improvement within a Scrum team.

Most importantly, this experience shifted my mindset from simply “building software” to delivering value through collaboration and adaptability. These lessons will continue to shape how I approach future technical and team-based projects.
